Overall Knowledge and Skills

All teachers require different knowledge and skills to effectively teach their students. Some knowledge and skills will be the same for all teachers; however, depending on what is being taught, the teacher needs additional skills and knowledge to most effectively teach their students their specific content areas. Teaching stage 4 technology mandatory in NSW schools is no exception.

Work Health and Safety

“Schools have a legal obligation in relation to safety. Teachers need to ensure that they comply with relevant legislation as well as system and school requirements in relation to safety and risk management when implementing their programs.” (NESA, 2017, p21).

Ensuring workplace health and safety is critical in all aspects of life, and due to the unconventional environment technology classes are in, being able to control the class to ensure the safety of everyone is key.

With this, a mandatory technology teacher is often competing with noisy equipment, conversation and the noise of collaboration. As a teacher, they need the skills and knowledge to gain the attention of the students when necessary for both safety and educational purposes.

Preparing Students for the Real World

We live in a constantly changing world. Technology is one of the fastest-changing areas. Teachers need to be aware of these changes and have the skills and knowledge to match the changes to best prepare students, including retraining to increase their skills. Ultimately the goal is to prepare students for life after school.

Implementation of Practical Experiences

Project-based learning and practical skills are included in all sections of the mandatory syllabus. This means that time management skills are even more crucial for a technology mandatory teacher to demonstrate this practice to the students, pack up class with plenty of time, and manage time so the students don’t have to stop mid-task.

“To satisfy the requirements of the syllabus, students must undertake a range of practical experiences that occupy the majority of course time.” (NESA, 2017, p21)
